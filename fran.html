Frankenstein book summary



"Frankenstein" is a novel written by Mary Shelley and published in 1818. It tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a young scientist who creates a monstrous creature in his laboratory. Victor Frankenstein becomes obsessed with the idea of creating life and succeeds in animating a creature made from body parts. But when he sees the monstrous result of his experiment, he is horrified and flees from his creation. The creature, rejected and abandoned by its creator, roams the world, seeking understanding and acceptance. However, it is met with fear and rejection from society, leading it to seek revenge against its creator. The creature's actions result in a series of tragic events, including the deaths of Victor's loved ones. Victor, plagued by guilt and remorse, becomes determined to destroy the creature. The novel follows Victor's pursuit of the creature across Europe, as he grapples with the consequences of his actions and the moral implications of playing God. The creature, in turn, confronts Victor and shares its own perspective on its miserable existence. The story delves into themes of ambition, science, morality, isolation, and the consequences of unchecked ambition. It also explores the nature of humanity, as both Victor and the creature struggle with their own sense of identity and purpose. As the novel reaches its climax, Victor's health deteriorates, and he becomes consumed by his desire for revenge against the creature. The story ends with a tragic and final confrontation between Victor and his creation in the Arctic wilderness. "Frankenstein" is a seminal work of gothic horror and science fiction that explores the ethical and moral implications of scientific progress and the consequences of playing with the forces of nature. It remains a timeless and thought-provoking novel that continues to captivate readers with its cautionary tale of ambition and hubris.
